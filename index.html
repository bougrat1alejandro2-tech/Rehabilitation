Athlete Stories

They Faced It.
They Fought Back.

These stories are shared with permission. Each one is different — different sports, different pressures, different roads to recovery. All of them are real.

⚡ Track & Field

Marion Jones

5× Olympic Medalist, Sprinter

At the peak of her career, Jones was the fastest woman in the world. Behind the medals was a secret — years of steroid use she later admitted to, leading to the surrender of all five Olympic medals and a prison sentence for lying to federal investigators.

"It was the biggest mistake of my life. I lost everything I had worked for."
In Recovery — Now a youth coach and advocate
⚾ Baseball

Ken Caminiti

1996 NL MVP, Third Baseman

In 2002, Caminiti became one of the first major athletes to publicly admit to steroid use during his MVP season. His openness sparked a national conversation about PEDs in baseball — but his battles with addiction ultimately claimed his life in 2004 at age 41.

"I've made a ton of mistakes. I don't think what I did was right, and I don't think it should be condoned."
Passed away 2004 — His story changed baseball forever
🚴 Cycling

David Millar

World Champion Cyclist

British cyclist David Millar admitted to EPO use in 2004 after a police raid. Banned for two years, he returned to the sport as one of cycling's most vocal anti-doping advocates and later helped found the Slipstream team, built entirely on clean sport principles.

"Doping was a form of self-destruction. Coming back clean was the hardest — and best — thing I ever did."
Full Recovery — Anti-doping campaigner & author
🏈 NFL

Lyle Alzado

All-Pro Defensive End

One of the NFL's most feared players, Alzado credited much of his performance to steroid use — a habit that began in college. In 1991, diagnosed with brain lymphoma, he publicly attributed his illness to steroid abuse, becoming a cautionary voice until his death in 1992.

"I'm not here to be judged. I just want to warn you."
Passed away 1992 — A defining warning for the NFL
🏊 Swimming

Jessica Hardy

Olympic Swimmer, World Record Holder

Hardy tested positive for clenbuterol in 2008, missing the Beijing Olympics. She successfully argued contaminated supplements as the source and served a reduced ban. She returned, competed at the 2012 London Olympics, and became an outspoken advocate for clean sport and supplement awareness.

"This experience defined my character in ways no gold medal ever could."
Full Recovery — Competed in 2012 & 2016 Olympics
💪 Bodybuilding

Rich Piana

Professional Bodybuilder & Entrepreneur

Piana was unusually candid about his 27-year steroid use, using his platform to discuss both the perceived benefits and the serious health consequences. His transparency was controversial — some praised his honesty, others felt it normalized dangerous behavior. He died in 2017 at age 46.

"If you have the choice to stay natural, stay natural. No trophy is worth dying for."
Passed away 2017 — His legacy sparked broader conversations
Education

Understanding
PEDs

Performance-enhancing drugs cover a broad category of substances athletes use to gain competitive advantages. They range from anabolic steroids and hormones to stimulants and blood doping agents.

Understanding what these substances are — and what they actually do to the body — is the first step toward making an informed choice, and toward recovery.

S

Anabolic Steroids

Synthetic testosterone variants that increase muscle mass and recovery. Examples: nandrolone, stanozolol. Long-term risks include heart disease, liver damage, hormonal disruption, and psychiatric effects.

H

Human Growth Hormone (HGH)

Promotes muscle growth and fat loss. Risks include acromegaly, joint pain, diabetes risk, and cardiovascular complications with long-term use.

E

EPO / Blood Doping

Erythropoietin boosts red blood cell production for endurance. Risks include stroke, heart attack, and death — especially during sleep when heart rate slows.

S

Stimulants

Amphetamines, cocaine, and similar substances increase alertness and reduce fatigue. Highly addictive with serious cardiovascular and psychological dependence risks.

B

Beta Blockers

Used in precision sports to reduce tremors and heart rate. Can cause dangerous cardiac suppression in healthy athletes and mask other substance use.

⚠ Documented Health Consequences

  • Premature cardiovascular disease and enlarged heart
  • Liver tumors and peliosis hepatis
  • Severe hormonal imbalances — lasting for years post-cessation
  • Psychological dependence, aggression, and depression
  • Tendon damage due to muscle outpacing connective tissue growth
  • Stunted growth in adolescent athletes
  • Increased risk of certain cancers with prolonged use
  • Compromised immune function
  • Withdrawal syndromes requiring medical supervision

The Pressure Is Real

Most athletes don't begin using PEDs out of moral weakness — they begin because of intense external pressure: coaches, teams, sponsorships, rankings. Acknowledging this doesn't excuse the choice, but it's essential context for recovery. Many athletes describe feeling like they had no option.

Recovery programs that ignore the systemic pressures athletes face are less effective. The best support approaches address both the substance dependency and the competitive environment that normalized use in the first place.

Recovery Roadmap

The Path
Forward

01

Acknowledge Without Shame

Recovery begins with honesty — with yourself first. PED use often develops gradually under immense pressure. Recognizing the problem isn't weakness; it's the foundation of every recovery story on this site.

02

Seek Medical Evaluation

Many PEDs cause physiological dependence. Stopping abruptly can be dangerous. A physician familiar with performance drug use can assess your health, manage withdrawal safely, and create a tapering plan.

03

Get Confidential Counseling

The psychological side of recovery is as critical as the physical. Sports-specialized therapists understand the identity pressures athletes face and can help you separate self-worth from performance.

04

Tell One Trusted Person

Isolation is the enemy of recovery. Whether a family member, former teammate, or therapist — having one person who knows what you're going through dramatically improves recovery outcomes.

05

Explore Your Options with Governing Bodies

In many cases, voluntary disclosure to sports governing bodies results in reduced sanctions, especially when paired with cooperation. USADA and WADA both have confidential inquiry processes.

06

Rebuild Your Identity

Athletes often define themselves entirely through performance. Recovery is also about rediscovering who you are beyond sport — and in many cases, becoming a more complete version of yourself.
